# ADHD Learning System

A small browser app for turning notes into active recall practice. Use it after reading, watching, listening, or practicing something so you can test whether you can explain the idea from memory.

## What This Is For

Use this app when you want to remember something better, check your understanding, or turn messy notes into quick quiz prompts.

Example:
I watched a video about working memory. Now I want to explain it without looking back.

## Step 1: Encode

Write what you learned in your own words. Messy is fine. The goal is to process the idea actively, not to create perfect notes.

Good example:
Working memory holds information temporarily while I use it. For example, when I do mental math, I hold numbers in my head while solving the problem. ADHD can make this harder because attention shifts away before the information gets used.

Starter format:
The main thing I learned is...
An example is...
This matters because...
I am still confused about...

## Step 2: Generate Questions

Click **Generate Questions** after writing a few full sentences. The app turns your notes into recall prompts.

Example note:
Photosynthesis is how plants use sunlight to make glucose from carbon dioxide and water.

Example question:
Explain this idea in your own words: Photosynthesis is how plants use sunlight to make glucose from carbon dioxide and water.

If the questions are weak, add clearer sentences, examples, and periods between ideas.

## Step 3: Retrieve

Answer each question from memory before rereading your notes. Even a partial answer helps because it shows what your brain can retrieve.

Example answer:
Photosynthesis lets plants turn light energy into food. They take in carbon dioxide and water, then produce glucose and oxygen.

If stuck, write a partial answer:
I remember this is about plants making food with sunlight, but I forgot the exact inputs and outputs.

Then check your notes and improve the answer.

## Step 4: Mark Reviewed

Click **Mark this as reviewed** after you have tried to answer from memory and corrected your answer.

Example:
I answered the working memory question, checked my notes, and added the part about attention shifting. Now I can mark it reviewed.

## Step 5: Reflect

Use the reflection box to decide what to review next.

Prompts:
- What did I misunderstand?
- How does this connect to what I already know?
- Where would I use this in real life?

Example reflection:
I mixed up working memory with long-term memory. This connects to why checklists help me finish tasks. I can use this by writing down multi-step tasks instead of trying to hold them all in my head.

## How to Know Your Active Recall Is Improving

Your active recall is improving when retrieving information becomes easier, faster, and more complete.

Signs of improvement:
- You can answer before looking at your notes.
- Your answers include more important details.
- You need fewer corrections after checking your notes.
- You can explain the idea using your own example.
- You remember the idea later the same day, the next day, or next week.
- You can connect the idea to something else you already know.

Example progress:
Session 1: Answered 1 out of 5 before looking. Missed key terms.
Session 2: Answered 3 out of 5 before looking. Remembered the main idea but missed one detail.
Session 3: Answered 5 out of 5 before looking. Used my own example. Ready to move on.

Simple progress check:
- How many questions did I answer before looking?
- What did I miss?
- Could I explain it with an example?
- What should I review next?

## Best Study Loop

Use short sessions.

1. Learn for 10-20 minutes.
2. Write 3-6 messy sentences.
3. Generate questions.
4. Answer without looking.
5. Correct your answers.
6. Write one next action.

Example session:
Topic: Photosynthesis
I answered 4 questions and missed chlorophyll. Next action: review what chlorophyll does.

## ADHD-Friendly Tips

Use a timer:
10 minutes learning
5 minutes writing
5 minutes retrieval
2 minutes reflection

Lower the bar:
I only need to write three messy sentences.

Make the next step tiny:
Review one confusing term, not the whole chapter.

## How to Know Your Active Recall Is Improving

Your active recall is improving when retrieving the information starts to feel easier, faster, and more complete.

Signs of improvement:

1. You can answer before looking
   At first, you may need to reread your notes often. Over time, you should be able to answer more prompts from memory first.

   Example:
   Earlier: “I know photosynthesis has something to do with sunlight, but I forgot the rest.”
   Later: “Photosynthesis uses sunlight, carbon dioxide, and water to make glucose and oxygen.”

2. Your answers include more details
   Improved recall does not mean memorizing exact wording. It means you can explain the idea with the important parts included.

   Example:
   Earlier: “Working memory helps you remember stuff.”
   Later: “Working memory temporarily holds information while I use it, like holding numbers in my head during mental math.”

3. You need fewer corrections
   At first, your answers may miss key terms or mix up concepts. Improvement means your first answer is closer to correct.

   Example:
   Earlier correction: “I forgot chlorophyll and glucose.”
   Later correction: “I only forgot to mention oxygen.”

4. You can explain the idea in a new way
   Strong recall means you can use your own examples, not just repeat the original note.

   Example:
   Original topic: working memory.
   New example: “Following a recipe uses working memory because I have to remember the current step while measuring ingredients.”

5. You remember it after time passes
   Try the same topic later the same day, tomorrow, or next week. If you can still explain it, your recall is getting stronger.

   Example tracking:
   Same day: needed notes twice.
   Next day: answered 3 out of 5 without notes.
   Next week: explained the main idea and one example without notes.

6. You can connect it to other things
   Recall is stronger when the idea connects to what you already know.

   Example:
   “Working memory connects to ADHD because external tools like checklists reduce how much information I need to hold in my head.”

Simple progress check:

After each session, write:

- How many questions did I answer before looking?
- What did I miss?
- Could I explain it with an example?
- What should I review next?

Example:

Session 1:
Answered 1/5 before looking. Missed key terms. Need to review chlorophyll.

Session 2:
Answered 3/5 before looking. Remembered glucose and oxygen. Still need to review chloroplasts.

Session 3:
Answered 5/5 before looking. Used my own example. Ready to move on.
